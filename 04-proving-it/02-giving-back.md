# Giving Back

## You Learn It Twice

There's a saying in teaching: you learn something once when you study it, and again when you teach it. The second time is deeper. When you explain something to someone who doesn't understand it yet, you find every gap in your own understanding. The parts you glossed over, the parts you memorized without really getting, the parts that seemed obvious until someone asked "why?"

In the traditional guild, late-stage apprentices helped train newer ones. Not because it was charity, but because it was the final stage of learning. If you can transfer a skill to someone else, you actually own it. If you can't, you were just following instructions.

## What This Looks Like

You're going to mentor a newer apprentice through part of their journey. This isn't optional. It's a requirement for the journeyman transition.

### Find Someone to Mentor

Look in the guild for apprentices who are in Phase 1 or Phase 2. They're learning the things you learned months ago. Offer to help. This can be informal ("I'll review your PRs and answer questions") or structured ("let's work through Phase 2 together, one session a week").

You don't need to be an expert. You need to be one step ahead, which you are. The best mentors aren't the ones who know everything. They're the ones who recently learned it and remember what was confusing.

### What You Do

**Review their work.** Look at their design docs, their code, their process documentation. Give specific, constructive feedback. Not "looks good" and not "this is all wrong." Point to specific things: "your design doc doesn't mention what happens when the API is down. What's your error handling plan?" This is the same adversarial review skill you've been developing, applied to someone else's learning.

**Answer their questions.** When they're stuck, help them get unstuck. But resist the urge to just give them the answer. Ask questions that lead them to it: "What did the error message say? What do you think that means? Have you tried asking the agent to explain it?" Teaching someone to solve problems is more valuable than solving their problems.

**Share what you learned the hard way.** The mistakes you made, the misconceptions you had, the things that clicked only after struggling with them. These are gold for someone who's about to make the same mistakes. "When I built my issue tracker, I let the agent put everything in one file and then spent two days refactoring. Here's what I'd do differently."

**Review their PRs on the guild toolkit.** When they submit their first PR, be one of the reviewers. You know the codebase. You know the conventions. Help them meet the standard.

### What You Don't Do

**Don't do their work for them.** If they're stuck on a feature, help them think through it. Don't write it for them or tell their agent what to prompt. They need to develop the skill, not borrow yours.

**Don't be harsh for the sake of it.** Adversarial review is valuable. Cruelty is not. The roast is about finding problems in code, not problems in people. Critique the work, not the person. "This function doesn't handle empty input" is useful. "How did you not think of empty input?" is not.

**Don't pretend to know things you don't.** If they ask a question you can't answer, say so. "I'm not sure, let's figure it out together" is a perfectly good response. Looking things up alongside someone is still mentoring.

## Document the Experience

Write a brief reflection after your mentoring period:

- Who did you mentor and through what part of the curriculum?
- What questions did they ask that made you rethink your own understanding?
- What was the hardest thing to explain? Why?
- What did you learn about your own knowledge gaps?
- What advice would you give to the next person who mentors someone?

This document goes in your portfolio. It's evidence of a skill that most people never develop: the ability to transfer knowledge effectively.

## Why This Matters Beyond the Guild

The ability to explain technical concepts to people who don't share your context is one of the most valuable skills in any technical career. It's how you write good documentation. It's how you onboard new team members. It's how you communicate with non-technical stakeholders. It's how you lead.

Most technical education completely ignores this. You learn to build things but not to explain them. The guild doesn't make that mistake. You build, and then you explain what you built, and then you help someone else build. The full cycle.
